## 2. What Went Well

1. **Party Mode pre-dev review catches portfolio-wide defects *before* code is written.** The 2.6 family review caught: a false architecture premise (VAL-10 — English is the volume path, not Filipino); a phantom table (G-2 couldn't close — `config_history` didn't exist); a κ-vs-α category error (named α libraries for a κ gate — would have silently redefined the gate on a non-interchangeable scale); an unreachable threshold (CP 95% LCB ≥ 0.95 @ n=30 fails even a perfect corpus at ~0.89); and an annotator-jurisdiction error (L1-native English imports US/UK defamation standards into a PH-libel-law corpus). Party Mode is the dominant scope-shaping force — and it is earning its cost.

2. **Honest-closure culture.** G-2 was *reopened* rather than nominally closed (2.6a → 2.10 phantom-table fallacy). INV-001 promoted yellow→green *exactly* at 2.1, not before. The stub-stack trap (2.9, 2.11) was named and refused — "a real test against stubs proves nothing." Perf numbers marked "TBD until non-stub serving path" rather than fabricated. A story carrying external blockers under a `ready-for-dev` label was flagged as *materially false* and split.

3. **Mechanical enforcement over convention.** The render gate, intake gate, and editorial log are type-enforced + lint-enforced + DB-enforced, not eyeball-enforced: write-only editorial log (repo API exposes no update/delete *and* PostgreSQL `REVOKE UPDATE, DELETE`); `req.auth` boundary (ESLint `no-restricted-syntax` *plus* branded TS type that cannot be constructed from `req.auth`); non-custodial signing (`makeEntry` takes a `getSignature` callback; server never holds private keys).

4. **The `sub`-based distinct-principal guard (2.3).** The two-person intake gate compares the JWT `sub` claim, not the key fingerprint — so one human holding two Ed25519 keys is still rejected. A subtle anti-coercion insight (SEC-7 insider threat).

5. **ADR as a contract-bearing artifact (2.5, ADR-024).** Not just a decision record — it carries 10 Chain Integrity Invariants, an Operational Runbook (fork detection, authoritative-fork determination, chain repair, forensic preservation), Co-Design Interface Contracts for downstream stories, and a Downstream Impact section listing every inheriting story. The ADR is the cross-story invariant-propagation mechanism.

6. **The fail-closed requirement threaded cleanly through three stories.** 2.7 *defined* it (OQ-29.6, the fresh-poll-per-`/query` strengthening was the panel-consensus patch that created the open question) → 2.9a *scoped it out* (resilience not correctness; pointed to 2.11) → 2.11 *implements* it (capstone) → 2.9b will *verify* it. A well-managed requirement→mechanism→verification arc — the traceability 2.7's AC #7 demanded.

7. **Party Mode intensity is justified on eval-gate work.** Every shipped eval-gate story went through 3 rounds × 5–6 agents and still produced multi-digit findings. The cost is high; the alternative is shipping defamation-grade software that passes its own gates while measuring the wrong thing.

8. **`pollAuditHealthForClaim()` naming (2.11).** Load-bearing semantics encoded in the function name — the "fresh poll per claim" invariant is unmissable at the call site. A reusable discipline: name safety-critical functions by their invariant, not their mechanism.

9. **Dual-layer boundary enforcement (2.2, 2.3, 2.4).** `req.auth` banned via lint *and* branded-type-banned at compile time; content hashes branded (`IntakeContentHash`/`CorpusHash`); editorial fields branded (`PrevHash`/`CorpusHash`/`Signature`/`Seq`). Compile-time + lint dual enforcement is the house style.

10. **Injectable-dependency factory pattern enables 100% Stryker (2.2).** `verifyJwt`/`createVerifyJwt` accept injected `ReplayDetector`/`AuthEventLogger`/`KeyRegistry` — the seam that made 100% mutation score achievable. Reuse for any security-critical function.

## 4. What Didn't Go Well / Challenges

1. **Story-size is systematically too large — orthogonal axes get packed into one story.** The 2.6 family split one story into six tracks across four orthogonal axes: schema-vs-eval, English-vs-Filipino, spec-vs-measure, intake-vs-config-surface. The original 2.9 packed golden-corpus (Epic 4), audit-death mechanism (didn't exist yet), and load characterization into one story. **Party Mode is doing the architectural decomposition that the spec-authoring phase should have done.** High-churn and high-value — but it means stories enter review fundamentally mis-scoped. *Root cause: spec-authors conflate orthogonal decisions (design-gate vs release-gate, volume vs salience, intake surface vs config surface) into one AC list.*

2. **The genesis-linkage bug recurred across two stories (2.4 → 2.5).** Editorial-log entry at seq=1 must chain off the genesis entry's *computed* `curr_hash`, not `GENESIS_PREV_HASH`. Fixed in 2.4; the same conceptual bug resurfaced in 2.5's `append()` and required re-fixing during code review. No canonical regression test traveled with the package. *Root cause: a subtle, load-bearing invariant with no traveling regression test.*

3. **CPU/hash "simulations" mistaken for chaos tests (2.4, 2.5).** Both stories shipped perf/chaos tests that were compute stubs, not real fault injection or DB load. 2.5's code review forced an expensive rebuild to real Testcontainers-backed tests (chaos 7/7, perf 6/6, with measured 1.80% exhaustion @ 50 writers). *Root cause: "tests exist" ≠ "tests exercise the intended scenario." A recurring quality-illusion theme — rhymes with 2.7's "arbitrary thresholds create the illusion of rigor."*

4. **The Acceptance Auditor review layer is unreliable (2.2, 2.4).** Failed/returned empty in 2 of the stories where it ran — likely context-overflow or tooling failures. One of our three adversarial review layers is silently dropping out, leaving a coverage hole in a defamation-grade pipeline.

5. **Stryker full-file ≥95% is currently unachievable on shared files (2.5).** The DoD had to be re-scoped *post-hoc* to a "concurrency slice" (100%, 66/66) because the full-file score is 63.59% with survivors in Story 2.4's paths (`queryLog`, `verifyChain` signature/time-range validation). The full-file run takes ~45 minutes. **There is no project-wide tiered-Stryker policy** — each story negotiates it ad hoc.

6. **Spec/code drift on error enumerations (2.5).** ADR-named error codes (`DUPLICATE_SEQUENCE`, `PREV_HASH_MISMATCH`, `SIGNING_CALLBACK_FAILED`) were absent from `EditorialErrorCode`. Tests silently aligned with different actual codes. No contract test links the spec union to the code union.

7. **The legal/compliance stakeholder persona is reframed late, during review (2.2, 2.5).** "Developer" → "compliance officer" / "legal reviewer" — every time, during party-mode, not at draft.

8. **Process-institution gaps carried from Epic 1 (P2, P3, A1).** We *behaved* better but didn't *institutionalize* it. Party Mode is the de facto default with no doc. "Implicit Prerequisites" sections never appeared. The ATDD scaffold pipeline wasn't operationalized for Epic 2. Behavior without institution erodes over time.

9. **Bash/shell robustness findings clustered heavily in 2.9a** (12 patches: unquoted paths → arrays, empty-string-as-unset, `grep -qx` vs `-Fxq`, fractional-UTC regex misflagging `.123Z`, `docker inspect` exit-status coercion, hardcoded timeouts). No other story had shell code — so the next shell-heavy story (2.9b's matrix-coverage) will repeat this without front-loaded scrutiny.

10. **Frontmatter/body status drift + filename/content mismatch.** 2.8 and 2.10 frontmatter said `ready-for-dev` while the body said `review`. The `2-9-unified-chaos-suite-500-rps-citation-invariant.md` file now holds 2.9a content (story_id/key are `2.9a`) — a latent confusion for anyone navigating by filename. Minor process hygiene, but erodes trust in the status field.

## 5. Key Insights

- **Party Mode pre-dev review is the highest-leverage process investment — and it is now load-bearing, not optional.** It caught 4 portfolio-wide defects this epic. The Epic 1 insight ("correlates directly with clean single-pass execution") is now strengthened to "catches architecture-level defects before they propagate." But it is still not formally documented as the default (P3 from Epic 1).

- **A deploy-blocking gate with an unreachable threshold is worse than no gate.** It guarantees a permanent RED or forces a dishonest vacuous green. The CP 95% LCB ≥ 0.95 @ n=30 failure is a one-line computation that two stories missed. **Every gate threshold needs a reachability check before shipping.**

- **"Fix once where it lives" for portfolio-wide defects.** The unreachable-threshold defect lived in ADR-0025 and propagated to ADR-0026 and `oq9.ts`. The fix was a single amendment to ADR-0025 (restructured into Part I protocol-spine + Part II instance), not peer-patches. The protocol-spine restructure converts every future language gate from "write a full peer ADR" into "write a thin instance record."

- **κ validates inter-rater consistency, NOT fidelity to a legal standard.** Annotator eligibility is what protects calibration to PH libel law; κ is orthogonal and cannot rescue a wrong-standard pool. This is a jurisdiction question, not a language question.

- **Invocation ≠ enforcement / the coverage-illusion theme.** 2.8's "Stryker category error" (100% mutation score on `gate.ts` doesn't prove the pipeline invokes the gate) rhymes with the stub-stack trap (a real test against stubs proves nothing) and 2.7's "arbitrary thresholds create the illusion of rigor." Across the epic there's a consistent insistence on *mechanical enforcement* over *nominal/asserted* guarantees.

- **Non-monotone rule transitions need banners + tracked catch-up.** The recalibrated OQ-9 pass rule is stricter on some axes, looser on others vs the old rule. The `oq9.ts` code catch-up is tracked as Open Item O-3 (4-part scope) with an honest "code catch-up" banner — not silently swapped. Silent swaps are how gates drift.

- **Story-size in this portfolio is systematically too large because spec-authors conflate orthogonal axes.** Volume vs salience, design-gate vs release-gate, intake-surface vs config-surface, spec-ADR vs deploy-blocking-code. Packing any two into one story triggers a split.

- **The stub-stack trap is a recurring, named anti-pattern (2.9, 2.11).** Both hit the same wall: `apps/api` and `audit-worker` are Story 1.1 `console.log` stubs, so a "real" integration/chaos test against the compose stack proves nothing. 2.9 was *split* over it; 2.11 *learned from it* and wired real components against injected fetch. The discipline: **never test a mechanism by exercising stubs; test real components against injected seams.**

- **Behavior without institution erodes.** Party Mode, the ATDD-as-spec pattern, and implicit-prerequisite surfacing all *happened* in Epic 2 — but P2, P3, and A1 from Epic 1 show that without written institutions, the practice is person-dependent and doesn't survive turnover.

## 11. Conclusion

Epic 2 delivered the editorial-integrity spine as *real, mechanically-enforced mechanisms*: the render gate is live, the editorial log is hash-chained and append-only, two-person intake is code-enforced, the audit-death fail-closed mechanism exists, and every output-affecting decision is reconstructable for a defamation defense. The citation-or-silence invariant graduated from yellow stub to green enforcement.

The process innovations from Epic 1 (Party Mode, Enforcement Boundary Tables, anti-hollow teeth) *scaled* — Party Mode caught four portfolio-wide defects this epic, including a false architecture premise and a category error that would have measured the wrong thing indefinitely. But the Epic 1 process-institution gaps (P2, P3, A1) persisted: we behaved better without writing it down. Behavior without institution erodes.

The primary risks to Epic 3 are the **stub-stack trap** (the real API server doesn't exist yet — TD1 is blocking), the **queue-substrate unknown** (largest technical risk; likely to force a split without a spike — TD4), and the **systematic story-sizing failure** (orthogonal-axis conflation — P1 + planning-review session). All three are addressed in the prep sprint and planning-review session before any Epic 3 feature story starts.

Epic 2 was executed with strong architectural discipline and an unusually honest closure culture: gates were reopened rather than nominally closed; stub-stack limitations were named and refused; portfolio-wide defects were fixed once where they lived. That culture is the epic's most valuable carry-forward asset.
