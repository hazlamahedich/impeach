# IIP — Customer Segments & Use Cases Research

**Subject:** Philippine Impeachment Intelligence Platform (IIP) — Market & Revenue-Bearing Customer Analysis
**Date:** June 2026
**Methodology:** Web-grounded desk research (RSF, PCIJ, CMFR, BGA, SyCipLaw, NCPAG, ADB, IFCN), cross-referenced with PH market reality. Honest assessment of willingness-to-pay.

---

## Executive Summary (read this first)

**The hard truth:** IIP's mission (transparency/accountability) and its revenue model are in tension. The *users* who most need IIP (Filipino citizens, underfunded newsrooms, advocacy NGOs) have the *least* capacity to pay. The *customers* with budget (Big Law, political-risk consultancies, foreign embassies, government agencies) need IIP less urgently and have alternative tools.

**Revenue-bearing reality (top-down):**
| Rank | Segment | Real revenue potential |
|------|---------|------------------------|
| 1 | Political-risk consultancies + foreign diplomatic/IFIs | **HIGH** — premium intelligence buyers |
| 2 | Big PH law law firms (SyCip, ACCRA, Romulo) | **HIGH** — Lexis-comparable budget |
| 3 | International correspondents + wire services | **MEDIUM** — bureaus have research budgets |
| 4 | Government (Senate PIO, Ombudsman, COA, SC Library) | **MEDIUM-HIGH** — slow but stable procurement |
| 5 | Academia (faculty + libraries, consortia) | **LOW-MEDIUM** — modest but recurring |
| 6 | PH major newsrooms (Rappler, PCIJ, VERA) | **LOW** — near-free / grant-funded seats |
| 7 | NGOs / civil society | **LOW** — grant-sponsored seats |
| 8 | Engaged citizens / students / diaspora | **NEAR-ZERO direct** — freemium/ad-supported only |

**Strategic implication:** IIP cannot survive on PH journalism or citizen subscriptions alone. A **cross-subsidy model is mandatory** — premium B2B seats (law firms + political risk + IFIs) fund free/subsidized access for journalists, NGOs, and the public. This is the same model ProPublica, OpenCorporates, and CQ Roll Call use.

## 1. Segment-by-Segment Analysis

### Segment 1 — International Political-Risk Consultancies & Diplomatic/IFI Missions
**Organizations:** Bower Group Asia, Eurasia Group, Verisk Maplecroft, Control Risks, Concordian, Concordia, Asia Group Advisors; embassies (US, EU, UK, Japan, Australia, Canada); IFIs (USAID, UNDP, EU Delegation, World Bank PH, ADB governance units, UN OHCHR, IFEX).

| Field | Finding |
|---|---|
| **Size (PH population)** | ~15–30 analyst seats across political-risk firms with PH coverage; ~30–50 diplomatic/IFI political-officer and governance-program seats in Manila. **Total addressable: ~50–100 seats.** |
| **Willingness to pay** | **PREMIUM.** These orgs routinely pay $5K–$25K/seat/yr for Bloomberg Terminal, Stratfor/RANE, IHS Markit, Frontier Strategy Group. IIP at $2K–$8K/seat/yr is a rounding error. |
| **Most compelling use case** | **Country/political-risk briefings** — Impeachment trajectories, senator-voting-pattern forecasts, conflict-of-interest mapping for client due diligence. BGA already publishes PH forecasts monthly. |
| **Go-to-market channel** | Direct enterprise sales; LinkedIn outreach to country-managers; demo in Makati/BCG Tower; referral from existing clients. **Single hire of one Manila-based biz-dev person** can crack this. |
| **Risks / barriers** | Small TAM (few dozen seats); high-touch sales cycle (3–6 months); they may build in-house if IIP doesn't move fast; requires English-only, polished UI. |
| **Priority tier** | **TIER 1** — highest revenue per seat, shortest list, highest willingness. Build-for-first. |

### Segment 2 — Big Philippine Law Firms
**Organizations:** SyCip Salazar, ACCRA Law, Romulo Mabanta Buenaventura Sayoc & De Los Angeles, Villaraza Angangco (CVP), Puno & Puno, PECruz, Gorriceta, Angara Abello; plus constitutional-law boutiques (e.g., the firms that defended/prosecuted Estrada, Corona, Duterte impeachments).

| Field | Finding |
|---|---|
| **Size (PH population)** | ~20–30 "Big Law" firms + ~50 constitutional-litigation boutiques; assume 5–15 lawyer seats per firm with impeachment/public-law practice. **Total addressable: ~300–600 seats.** |
| **Willingness to pay** | **HIGH.** Already pay Lexis Asia, Thomson Reuters Westlaw Asia, Supreme Court eLibrary subscriptions. **$1K–$5K/seat/yr** is the realistic IIP wedge. |
| **Most compelling use case** | **Constitutional litigation prep + impeachment jurisprudence research.** Estrada (2000–01) and Corona (2012) cases generated landmark doctrine; lawyers need to Shepardize these precedents. IIP's contradiction detection + evidence map is a unique wedge Lexis lacks. |
| **Go-to-market channel** | Direct sales to firm librarians/Knowledge-Management partners; CLE (Continuing Legal Education) partnerships with IBP (Integrated Bar of the Philippines); demo at PH Bar Association events. |
| **Risks / barriers** | LexisNexis/Thomson already entrenched; "library culture" is slow to adopt; managing partners decide, not associates; long sales cycle (6–12 months). Smaller litigation boutiques are the easier wedge. |
| **Priority tier** | **TIER 1** — top revenue per seat after political-risk. Build the legal-vertical module early. |

### Segment 3 — International Correspondents & Wire Services (Manila Bureaus)
**Organizations:** Reuters Manila, AFP Manila, AP, BBC, NHK World, Bloomberg, Financial Times, NYT (stringers), dpa, Kyodo, Xinhua; plus AFP Fact Check PH, dpa fact-check.

| Field | Finding |
|---|---|
| **Size (PH population)** | ~40–60 accredited foreign correspondents in Manila (foreign correspondents club) + ~20 fact-checkers. **Total addressable: ~60–100 seats** at bureau-level institutional pricing. |
| **Willistatement to pay** | **MEDIUM.** International bureaus have research budgets PH newsrooms lack. Reuters/AFP/AP can pay $500–$2K/seat/yr as bureau expense. Fact-check arms funded by Google News Initiative / Meta (third-party fact-checking program). |
| **Most compelling use case** | **Fact-checking claims + source verification + building timeline graphics for international audiences.** Foreign correspondents covering PH impeachment for global readers need fast context they can't get elsewhere in English. |
| **Go-to-market channel** | Pitch to bureau chiefs directly (Manila is small; FOCAP — Foreign Correspondents Association of the PH — is the gateway); offer free bureau trials; co-publish with one wire to demonstrate value. |
| **Risks / barriers** | Limited seat count; bureaus may share logins; Meta/Google fact-check funding is *cyclic* (Google cut fact-check funding 2023–24). |
| **Priority tier** | **TIER 1.5** — strong credibility play (Reuters byline citing IIP = marketing) and decent budget. Not pure revenue but high strategic signal value. |

### Segment 4 — Government (the paradoxical buyer)
**Organizations:** Senate Public Information Office (publishes the transcripts IIP ingests), House Committee on Justice, Office of the Ombudsman research, COA Legal, COMELEC Law Dept, Supreme Court Library, OSG (Office of the Solicitor General), DOJ.

| Field | Finding |
|---|---|
| **Size (PH population)** | ~200–500 government researcher/lawyer/librarian seats across 8–10 agencies. **Total addressable: ~300–500 seats** under multi-year GAA-funded contracts. |
| **Willingness to pay** | **MEDIUM-HIGH (contract value, not per-seat).** PH government procures via PPMP/GAA. Realistic ICP-style contract: **₱3M–₱15M/yr (~$50K–$260K) per agency**, multi-year. |
| **Most compelling use case** | **Cross-agency reference + public-information service.** Senate PIO can use IIP to power its own public-facing trial tracker (re-publishing); Ombudsman can cross-reference Senate testimony vs. its own case files. |
| **Go-to-market channel** | **GovTech procurement is relationship-driven.** Must register with PhilGEPS; need a PH-registered entity; pitch to agency IT/Undersecretary level; get one lighthouse agency (likely Senate PIO or Ombudsman) then expand. Budget alignment needed before September GAA cycle. |
| **Risks / barriers** | **6–18 month procurement cycle**; requires PH-registered legal entity; "build-it-ourselves" temptation; political risk (a hostile Senate President could cancel); anti-corruption procurement rules favor lowest bidder — IIP must justify premium via TOR specs. |
| **Priority tier** | **TIER 2** — slow but stable and large. Engage early (Year 1) but don't depend on it for cash flow. |

### Segment 5 — Academia / Research / Think Tanks
**Organizations:** UP NCPAG (confirmed: 4 research centers + library + grad programs), Ateneo School of Government, DLSU Jesse Robredo School, UST Law, Arellano Law, Lyceum School of Law, San Beda Law; think tanks Makati Business Club, AER, IBON, Stratbase ADRi, Pulse Asia, SWS; journals Philippine Political Science Journal, Philippine Law Journal, Ateneo Law Journal.

| Field | Finding |
|---|---|
| **Size (PH population)** | ~10–15 PH universities with relevant pol-sci/law/public-admin programs × ~20–50 faculty/PhD researchers each = ~300–500 faculty seats; ~5,000–8,000 graduate/law students as occasional users. **Realistic paying seats: 300–500 faculty + library consortium deals.** |
| **Willingness to pay** | **LOW-MEDIUM.** Standard academic-license band: **$50–$500/seat/yr**. Library/consortium deals (CHED-funded, e.g.) **$5K–$25K/yr per consortium.** |
| **Most compelling use case** | **Peer-reviewed research + dissertation support + comparative-politics scholarship.** NCPAG's Centers of Excellence literally do this work. Impeachment jurisprudence = a recurring law-review topic. |
| **Go-to-market channel** | Library consortia (via DOST-PCIEERD, CHED, or individual library directors); faculty word-of-mouth; sponsor the Philippine Political Science Association annual conference; offer free .edu.ph seats to seed adoption. |
| **Risks / barriers** | "Library culture" (slow adoption, peer-review cycles); CHED budget cuts; faculty prefer free sources (SCRA, LawPhil); UP culture resists paid tools. |
| **Priority tier** | **TIER 2** — strategically important (legitimacy, peer-reviewed citations raise IIP's authority) but low direct revenue. Pursue consortium deals, not individual faculty. |

**Organizations:** Rappler, PCIJ, VERA Files, Inquirer.net, PhilStar, GMA News Online, ABS-CBN News, Manila Bulletin, SunStar network, Philippine Press Institute (PPI) member papers (~60 community papers), CMFR, Philippines graphic, Bulatlat, Kodao, Pinoy Weekly.

| Field | Finding |
|---|---|
| **Size (PH population)** | ~15 major national outlets × ~5–15 journalist seats with investigative remit = ~150–250 seats at majors; PPI community papers ~60 outlets × 2–3 seats = ~150 community seats. **Total addressable: ~300–400 journalism seats.** |
| **Willistatement to pay** | **LOW.** RSF 2026 confirms PH independent media "economic viability is uncertain," regional papers "struggling to survive." Realistic willingness: **₱0 (free) to ~₱500–₱2,000/seat/mo (~$9–$35)** — *and only at the majors.* Community papers = $0. |
| **Most compelling use case** | **Lead-finding, contradiction detection, fact-checking claims.** PCIJ's own beat (corruption, dynasties, ICC, campaign finance) overlaps IIP exactly. CMFR's *Journalism Review* would use IIP's media-comparison feature weekly. |
| **Go-to-market channel** | **Do not charge PH newsrooms directly.** Pursue: (a) Google News Initiative / Knight Foundation / NMF grants to underwrite free journalism seats; (b) PPI partnership for community-paper distribution; (c) co-publication model (PCIJ/VERA co-byline using IIP) = organic adoption; (d) National Union of Journalists of the PH (NUJP) endorsement. |

| **Priority tier** | **TIER 1 (mission-critical, not revenue-critical)** — must be free/subsidized; treated as **marketing + legitimacy channel**, not revenue. A Rappler/PCIJ byline citing IIP is worth more than ₱50K/yr in subscriptions. |

## 3. Tiered Customer Priority

### TIER 1 — Build For First (Year 1)
These segments pay the bills. Optimize UI/sales for these.

| Segment | Why Tier 1 | Pricing band |
|---|---|---|
| Political-risk consultancies + IFIs | Highest ARPU ($2K–$8K/seat/yr); smallest sales list (~80 accounts); fastest cycle. | Premium |
| Big PH law firms | Already pay Lexis; $1K–$5K/seat/yr realistic; ~300–600 seats. | High |
| Intl correspondents / wire bureaus | Moderate ARPU + **massive credibility halo** (Reuters/AFP citations). | Medium |

**Combined Tier-1 realistic revenue (Year 2):** 200–400 paid seats × blended $2K/seat/yr = **$400K–$800K/yr**. This is the floor of a fundable/sustainable operation.

### TIER 2 — Expand Into (Years 2–3)
Slower sales cycles, lower ARPU, but recurring and strategically important.

| Segment | Why Tier 2 | Pricing band |
|---|---|---|
| Government (Senate PIO, Ombudsman, COA, SC Lib) | Big contracts ($50K–$260K/agency/yr) but 6–18 mo procurement. | Contract |
| Academia (consortia + faculty) | Low ARPU but legitimacy + grant eligibility. | Low |
| NGOs (donor-sponsored seats) | Near-zero direct; funded via USAID/NED/EU grants. | Sponsored |
| Freelance/podcast journalists | Free; PR value. | Free |

**Combined Tier-2 realistic revenue (Year 3):** $200K–$500K/yr (mostly government + academic consortia + one donor-sponsored program).

### TIER 3 — Nice To Have / Mission (Ongoing)
Cannot fund the platform; do not optimize for them.

| Segment | Why Tier 3 | Pricing band |
|---|---|---|
| Engaged citizens | Freemium, ads, donations. ₱0–₱499/mo. | Free/Ad |
| Students | Free via .edu.ph auth; cap queries. | Free |
| Diaspora | Freemium; potential small subscription volume. | Freemium |

**Realistic Tier-3 revenue:** <$50K/yr direct; value is in **impact metrics for grant reports** and **traffic for ad/sponsorship**.

## 6. Critical Risks & Mitigations

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| PH newsroom budgets never recover | **High** | Medium | Don't depend on them; treat as PR channel only. |
| Government contract cancelled after political turnover | Medium | High | Diversify across multiple agencies + private sector. |
| LexisNexis/Thomson launch competing PH impeachment module | Low-Medium | High | Win the contradiction-detection + evidence-mapping wedges Lexis can't easily replicate. |
| IIP seen as partisan (pro-anti any faction) | **High** | Critical | Strict transparency on methodology; non-partisan advisory board (CMFR, NCPAG, IBP); publish data sources publicly. |
| Free journalism tier abused by state-aligned influencers | Medium | High | NUJP/IFCN credential verification; rate limits; abuse-report system. |
| Red-tagging of IIP staff / NGO partners | Medium | Critical | Insurance, legal retainer, international fiscal sponsorship (e.g., 501c3 via NCMEC-style setup) for staff safety. |
| Procurement cycle slips (gov) | High | Medium | Don't budget for gov revenue in Year 1. |
| Citizen/diaspora willingness-to-pay disappoints | High | Low | Was always freemium anyway; pivot to ads/sponsorship. |

---

## 7. Bottom Line — The Three Decisions That Matter

1. **Price for the top, give to the bottom.** Premium B2B (law firms, political-risk, IFIs, government) funds free access for journalism, NGOs, and the public. There is no other sustainable model for a transparency product in the Philippines.

2. **The legal-vertical and political-risk-vertical modules are the real product.** NL Q&A over PDFs is a feature, not a business. The wedges that command premium pricing are **contradiction detection, evidence mapping with citations, senator dashboards, and API access.**

3. **Mission is the moat, not the model.** The single largest long-term asset IIP can build is *reputation* — being cited by Reuters, PCIJ, UP NCPAG, FLAG. That reputation is what allows premium B2B sales to close. **Protect the brand at all costs.** One partisan scandal kills the whole thing.
