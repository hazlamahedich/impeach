## 1. Constitutional Framework

### 1.1 Source provision
The entire impeachment architecture is anchored in **Article XI ("Accountability of Public Officers"), Sections 2–8 and 17** of the **1987 Constitution**.
_Source: https://www.chanrobles.com/article11.htm (full verbatim text)_

### 1.2 Who may be impeached (Art. XI, Sec. 2) — exclusive list
The Constitution enumerates a **closed, exclusive** roster of impeachable officers; it cannot be enlarged by statute:
- The **President**
- The **Vice-President**
- **Members of the Supreme Court** (Chief Justice + 14 Associate Justices)
- **Members of the Constitutional Commissions**: Civil Service Commission (CSC), Commission on Elections (COMELEC), Commission on Audit (COA)
- The **Ombudsman** *(Note: Deputies are NOT on the list — a gap litigated in the Sereno era.)*

_Source: https://www.chanrobles.com/article11.htm | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_Philippines_
**Confidence: HIGH** (verbatim constitutional text)

### 1.3 Exclusive grounds (Art. XI, Sec. 2) — six enumerated
1. **Culpable violation of the Constitution** — must be "culpable" (worthy of punishment); excludes good-faith/unintentional breaches.
2. **Treason** — as defined in Revised Penal Code Art. 114 (levying war / adhering to enemies).
3. **Bribery** — direct (RPC Art. 210) or indirect (RPC Art. 211).
4. **Graft and corruption** — e.g., violations of RA 3019 (Anti-Graft and Corrupt Practices Act), unexplained wealth.
5. **Other high crimes** — serious crimes against the State; *Francisco Jr. v. House of Representatives* (2003) held that defining this term is a **non-justiciable political question** for Congress.
6. **Betrayal of public trust** — added only in the 1987 Constitution; broad catch-all covering malfeasance, inexcusable negligence, cronyism, favoritism, abuse of power that brings the office into disrepute (per 1986 ConCom records).

_Source: https://www.chanrobles.com/article11.htm | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_Philippines_
**Confidence: HIGH** (constitutional text + ConCom deliberations)

### 1.4 The one-year bar (Art. XI, Sec. 3[5])
> "No impeachment proceedings shall be initiated against the same official more than once within a period of one year."

The Supreme Court has interpreted "initiation" extensively (see §3.3 below). **Confidence: HIGH**

### 1.5 SALN duty (Art. XI, Sec. 17)
Every public officer must file, under oath, a **Statement of Assets, Liabilities, and Net Worth** upon assumption of office and annually thereafter. For the President, VP, Cabinet, Congress, SC, Constitutional Commissions, and AFP generals/flag officers, the SALN **must be disclosed to the public**. Non-disclosure was the core charge in *Corona* (2012) and a charge in *Sereno* (2018) and the *Duterte* impeachment (2025).
_Source: https://www.chanrobles.com/article11.htm | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statement_of_assets,_liabilities,_and_net_worth_
**Confidence: HIGH**

## 2. Procedural Lifecycle (House → Senate)

The Constitution (Art. XI, Sec. 3) sets the framework; the **House Rules of the 19th/20th Congress** and the **Senate Rules of Procedure in Impeachment Trials (as of March 2026)** supply the granular mechanics.
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_Philippines (citing House rules §124–129 and Senate rules §85–96)_
**Confidence: HIGH** on thresholds; **MEDIUM** on exact rule numbers (secondary source).

### Step 1 — Filing of Verified Complaint
A "verified complaint" (sworn under oath, based on personal knowledge or authentic records) may be filed via **three modes**:
1. By **any Member of the House of Representatives**;
2. By **any private citizen**, **provided** the complaint is **endorsed by a Member of the House** via resolution (the "IBP-certified letter" route refers to citizen complaints channelled through the Integrated Bar of the Philippines);
3. By a **resolution of impeachment signed by at least one-third (1/3) of all House Members** — this mode **bypasses the Committee** and is **automatically constituted as the Articles of Impeachment**, transmitted directly to the Senate.

_File destination:_ Office of the Secretary-General of the House → transmitted to the Speaker.
_Source: Art. XI, Sec. 3(2); https://www.chanrobles.com/article11.htm | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_Philippines_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### Step 2 — Referral Timelines (Art. XI, Sec. 3[2])
- The Speaker must include the complaint in the **Order of Business within 10 session days**.
- Within **3 session days** thereafter, the complaint must be **referred to the House Committee on Justice**.
- **Definition of "session day"** (post-2026 SC ruling in the Duterte case): any calendar day on which the House actually convenes and holds a session (rejecting the old "one session day can span multiple calendar days" theory).
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_Philippines_
**Confidence: HIGH** on the 10/3/60-day counts; **MEDIUM** on the 2026 SC "session day" definition (recent jurisprudence).

### Step 3 — Committee Determinations (three sequential tests)
The House Committee on Justice sequentially evaluates:
1. **Sufficiency in form** — proper signature, verification under oath, House endorsement. *If insufficient → returned to Secretary-General within 3 session days (dismissal).*
2. **Sufficiency in substance** — assuming allegations true, do they state impeachable offenses within the Committee's jurisdiction? *If insufficient → dismissed.*
3. **Sufficiency of grounds** — considering complaint + answer + reply + rejoinder together, is there a basis to proceed?

**Pleading sequence upon passing form/substance:** notice to respondent → **10 days to file Answer** (no motion to dismiss allowed) → complainant **3 days to Reply** → respondent **3 days to Rejoinder**. Affidavits and documentary evidence attach to each pleading.
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_Philippines (citing House Rules §124–127)_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### Step 4 — Hearing Proper (probable cause determination)
- Committee may issue **subpoenas** to compel witnesses/documents; chair may cap direct/cross-examination time.
- Purpose: determine **probable cause** to impeach.
- Vote: **majority of ALL Committee members** (not just those present).
- Outcome: Committee report + resolution to plenary, **either** (a) with Articles of Impeachment (favorable) **or** (b) recommending dismissal.
- **Timeline:** Committee must report within **60 session days** of referral.
_Source: Art. XI, Sec. 3(2); https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_Philippines_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### Step 5 — Plenary Vote (Art. XI, Sec. 3[3])
- Calendared for plenary consideration within **10 session days** of committee report receipt.
- **Threshold: at least one-third (1/3) of ALL House Members** (i.e., ≈ **102 of 306** in the current 20th Congress; was 95 of 285 in the 15th Congress during *Corona*).
  - If committee report is **favorable** → 1/3 affirms → **Articles of Impeachment adopted**.
  - If committee recommended **dismissal** → 1/3 **overrides** → Committee forced to draft Articles.
  - Failure to reach 1/3 either way → **complaint dismissed**.
- **Roll-call vote**; each Member's vote recorded in the Journal.
_Source: Art. XI, Sec. 3(3); https://www.chanrobles.com/article11.htm_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### Step 6 — Service / Transmittal to Senate
- Articles of Impeachment **physically served** on the Senate by a House prosecution delegation (e.g., in *Corona*, Tupas & Umali delivered them Dec. 13, 2011).
- The Constitution mandates trial by the Senate shall **"forthwith proceed"** (Art. XI, Sec. 3[4]).
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_of_Renato_Corona_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### Step 7 — Senate Convenes as Impeachment Court (Senate Rules §85–86)
- **Presiding officer:**
  - **President on trial → Chief Justice presides, but does NOT vote.**
  - **Any other official → Senate President presides and votes.**
- Senate President formally requests the Chief Justice's attendance at a specified time/place when the President is the respondent.
- **Presiding officer takes an oath**, then administers to all senators the oath to do "impartial justice according to the Constitution and laws."
- Senators must **observe political neutrality** (duty exercised "without unfair discrimination and regardless of party affiliation").
- **House prosecution panel = 11 Members** of the House, who may be **assisted by private prosecutors** under their direct supervision.
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_Philippines (citing Senate Rules §85–86, 96)_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### Step 8 — Summons & Pleadings (Senate Rules §88–89)
- Writ of **summons** issued to respondent, **incorporating the Articles of Impeachment**.
- Respondent has a **non-extendible 10 days** to file an **Answer**.
- Prosecution has **non-extendible 5 days** to file a Reply.
- Failure to appear/answer → trial proceeds as if **"not guilty"** plea entered.
- A **"guilty"** plea → Senate may enter judgment **without further proceedings**.
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_Philippines (citing Senate Rules §88–89)_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### Step 9 — Conduct of Trial (Senate Rules §90–92, 94)
- Trial commences at **2:00 p.m.** (or other designated hour); Senate suspends regular legislative business.
- **Doors remain open to the public**; visitors must observe silence or face ejection.
- **Opening statements:** limited to **1 representative per side**.
- Witnesses **sworn under a specific oath** (Rule 94).
- **Direct examination by 1 counsel; cross-examination by 1 opposing counsel.**
- **Senator-judges may actively question** witnesses/prosecutors/defense — limited to **2 minutes per senator**.
- All motions/objections addressed to the presiding officer.
- **Interlocutory disputes:** max **1 hour per side** to argue.
- **Closing arguments:** up to **2 persons per side**; House opens and closes.
- The **Rules of Court apply *mutatis mutandis*** (evidence rules, etc.).
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_Philippines (citing Senate Rules §90–94)_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### Step 10 — Voting & Judgment (Art. XI, Sec. 3[6]–[7]; Senate Rules §92–93)
- Senate must **complete trial of ALL articles before any voting**.
- Each article voted on **separately**, by **roll call**.
- Each senator must **rise and verbally declare "guilty" or "not guilty"**; may use up to **2 minutes** to explain.
- **Conviction threshold: two-thirds (2/3) of ALL Senators** (i.e., **16 of 24**; in *Corona* the vote was **20–3** on Article II).
- Conviction on **any single article** → **immediate removal** + **perpetual disqualification** from holding any office under the Republic.
- Acquittal if **no article** reaches 2/3.
- **No motion for reconsideration** of the final vote permitted — decision is **final**.
- **Sanction scope (Art. XI, Sec. 3[7]):** judgment **does not extend beyond removal + disqualification**; the convicted party **remains liable to criminal/civil prosecution** in regular courts.
_Source: Art. XI, Sec. 3(6)–(7); https://www.chanrobles.com/article11.htm | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_of_Renato_Corona_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### Step 11 — Judicial Review (limited but real)
Although the Senate is "the sole judge" (Art. XI, Sec. 3[6]), the Supreme Court has asserted **expanded *certiorari* jurisdiction** (Art. VIII, Sec. 1) to review **grave abuse of discretion** in impeachment proceedings:
- ***Francisco Jr. v. House of Representatives*** (2003) — defined "initiation" for the one-year bar.
- ***Gutierrez v. House of Representatives*** (2011) — allowed consolidation of near-simultaneous complaints.
- **2025–2026 Duterte rulings** — SC voided the 4th impeachment complaint on one-year-bar grounds and redefined "session day."
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_Philippines_
**Confidence: HIGH** that SC review exists; **MEDIUM** on specifics of the 2026 ruling (very recent, based on secondary reporting).

## 4. Senate Impeachment Rules — Key Provisions Affecting Document Structures

The **Senate Rules of Procedure in Impeachment Trials** (current version: **March 2026**) govern the trial phase. Rule numbers below are as cited in secondary literature (the official PDF at senate.gov.ph returned HTTP 403).
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_Philippines (citing Senate Rules §85–96)_
**Confidence: MEDIUM** (rule numbers via secondary citation; content is well-attested across cases).

| Rule | Subject | Document-Structure Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **§85** | Presiding officer designation; CJ presides (no vote) over presidential trials; oath of presiding officer | Verdicts in presidential trials signed by CJ + Senate President; non-presidential trials by Senate President only. |
| **§86** | Senator-judge oath of "impartial justice"; **political neutrality** mandate | Each verdict records that senator-judges were sworn. |
| **§88** | **Summons** to respondent **incorporating the Articles of Impeachment**; **10-day** Answer; **5-day** Reply | The **Articles of Impeachment become an annex to the Senate summons** — a key document-attachment convention. |
| **§89** | Default = "not guilty" plea; "guilty" plea → judgment without trial | A **guilty plea** produces a one-line judgment, not a reasoned decision. |
| **§90** | Trial at 2 p.m.; **Secretary of the Senate records proceedings** | **Senate Journal of the impeachment trial** is the canonical record (not a court stenographer's transcript). |
| **§91** | **Open doors**; direct/cross-exam by single counsel each; **senator-judges may question (2 min each)**; all motions to presiding officer | Transcripts interleave senator-judge questions — a distinct structural feature vs. regular trials. |
| **§92** | Opening statements (1/side); closing arguments (2/side); **vote on ALL articles before voting**; **2/3 conviction**; **no motion for reconsideration** | The **verdict is a roll-call tally per article**, not a unified reasoned opinion. Finality is built in. |
| **§93** | Roll-call vote; **"guilty"/"not guilty"**; up to **2 min explanation per senator** | Each senator's explanation may be **appended to the Journal** — a rich source of individual reasoning for KG ingestion. |
| **§94** | **Witness oath** (specific wording) | Witness testimony carries a verbatim oath form. |
| **§96** | Oath administration procedure | — |

**Evidence rules:** The **Revised Rules of Court apply *mutatis mutandis*** — so exhibits are numbered (Prosecution Exh. "A," "B"...; Defense Exh. "1," "2"...), markers like "A-1" for sub-markings, and testimonial records follow standard Philippine judicial conventions. **Hearsay, authentication, and best-evidence rules apply**, but senator-judges may relax them in their discretion (as seen in *Corona* with the AMLC bank records).
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_of_Renato_Corona_
**Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH**

## 5. Document Conventions

### 5.1 Articles of Impeachment — Standard Structure
Based on the **Corona Articles (Dec. 12, 2011)** as the canonical model:
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_of_Renato_Corona#Articles_of_Impeachment_
**Confidence: HIGH**

1. **Caption / Title:** "Articles of Impeachment against [Name], [Office]"
2. **Preamble:** identifies the House of Representatives acting through its Members.
3. **Numbered Articles** (typically **Roman numerals** I–VIII), each containing:
   - **A statement of the specific act/omission** (often a paragraph-long factual narrative);
   - **A citation of the constitutional/statutory ground violated** (e.g., "betrayal of public trust and/or culpable violation of the Constitution");
   - A closing formula: *"Wherefore, respondent should be impeached."*
4. **Verification & Authentication:** signed by ≥1/3 of House Members (when via the auto-Articles route) or by the Speaker + Secretary-General (when via Committee report + plenary vote).
5. **Service block:** addressed to the President of the Senate.

**Note on the Estrada complaint:** 115 signatures (≥85 = 1/3 threshold of the then-265-seat House) made it **self-executing Articles** without a Committee hearing — the fastest path on record.
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidency_of_Joseph_Estrada (Impeachment proceedings section)_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### 5.2 Senate Verdict — Structure
The Senate verdict is **NOT a unified reasoned opinion** like an SC decision. It is:
- A **roll-call tally** per article (e.g., *"Article II: Guilty — 20; Not Guilty — 3"*), recorded in the **Senate Journal**;
- Each senator-judge's **2-minute explanation** (optional) may be appended;
- A **short order** signed by the presiding officer (CJ for presidential trials) pronouncing **"judgment of conviction"** or **"acquittal"**, ordering removal and disqualification upon conviction.
- The **fallo** is read aloud by the presiding officer's clerk.

In *Corona*, the verdict document (May 29, 2012) was short; the substantive reasoning lives in the **20 individual senator explanations** and the trial transcript.
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_of_Renato_Corona#Decision_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### 5.3 Supreme Court Decision — Philippine Citation & Precedent Conventions
Philippine SC decisions follow a recognizable structure relevant to KG extraction:
- **Caption:** *Case Title* (e.g., *Francisco Jr. v. House of Representatives*, *Republic v. Sereno*), **G.R. No.** (General Register number), **date**.
- **Ponente** (the writing Justice) named at the top.
- **Facts → Issues → Ruling → Dispositive (*fallo*)** structure.
- **Citation style:** Decisions cite earlier G.R. numbers; the **SCRA** (Supreme Court Reports Annotated) and **PHIL** reporters are the official series.
- **Doctrine of *stare decisis*** applies (Art. VIII, Sec. 1, 1987 Constitution + *stare decisis* jurisprudence).
- Decisions are published on **sc.judiciary.gov.ph** and **lawphil.net** (the latter a reliable mirror).

**Manila Reporter style note:** The phrase "Manila Reporter style" is not a recognized Philippine citation manual; PH citations follow the **Philippine Reports / SCRA** convention or Bluebook-style adaptations. _Confidence: MEDIUM_ on this specific term.

### 5.4 SALN — Standardized Form
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statement_of_assets,_liabilities,_and_net_worth | Official Gazette SALN portal_
**Confidence: HIGH**

- **Legal basis:** Art. XI, Sec. 17 (Constitution) + **Sec. 8, RA 6713** (Code of Conduct & Ethical Standards for Public Officials).
- **Form:** A **standardized CSC (Civil Service Commission) template** with sections for:
  - **Assets** (real property: description, kind, location, year/mode of acquisition, assessed value, fair market value, acquisition cost; personal property; co-mingled assets);
  - **Liabilities**;
  - **Net worth**;
  - **Business interests & financial connections**;
  - **Relatives in government** (conflict-of-interest disclosure).
- **Scope:** Filer + **spouse** + **unmarried children under 18 living at home**.
- **Filing cycle:** upon assumption of office + annually **on or before April 30**.
- **Custodians (six):** Ombudsman (for President, VP, governors, mayors, constitutional body heads); Office of the President (Cabinet); CSC (rank-and-file); House Secretary-General (Cong.); Chief Justice / SC En Banc Clerk (Justices); COA (others).
- **Public access:** Presumptively open (Sec. 8[b], RA 6713) but **regulated** — Ombudsman Memo Circular No. 1, s. 2020 (under Martires) restricted access; **Ombudsman Remulla lifted the restrictions on Oct. 14, 2025.** SC ruled in 2021 that custodians may set reasonable conditions.
- **Retention:** 10 years.

## 6. Defamation / Libel Risk for AI Platforms

A KG platform that surfaces **claims and allegations** about living public figures (politicians, witnesses, respondents) faces **two overlapping liability regimes** in the Philippines.

### 6.1 Revised Penal Code — Ordinary Libel (Arts. 353–362)
- **Art. 353** defines libel: a public and malicious imputation of a crime, vice, or defect (real or imaginary) that tends to dishonor/discredit a person.
- **Art. 355** — written/printed libel (covers published text).
- **Penalty:** *prisión correccional* in its **minimum or medium** periods = **6 months to 4 years 2 months**.
- **Prescriptive period:** **1 year** (Act 3326 / RPC).
- **Defenses:** truth (if published with good motives/justifiable ends), privileged communication, fair report of official proceedings.
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cybercrime_Prevention_Act_of_2012 (background on RPC libel)_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### 6.2 Cybercrime Prevention Act (RA 10175) — Cyberlibel §4(c)(4)
- **Enacted:** Sept. 12, 2012; **effective** Oct. 3, 2012.
- **§4(c)(4):** criminalizes libel committed via "a computer system or any other similar means."
- **Penalty:** *prisión mayor* = **6 to 12 years** (one degree higher than RPC libel).
- **Prescriptive period:** **12 years** (per Act 3326, since RA 10175 doesn't specify — affirmed in the Ressa appellate ruling).
- **Constitutionality upheld:** ***Disini v. Secretary of Justice*** (G.R. No. 203335, **Feb. 18, 2014**) — but the SC **struck down** the portion of §4(c)(4) penalizing **mere recipients/reactors** (liking/sharing) as unconstitutional; only the **original author/publisher** is liable.
- **Universal jurisdiction:** applies to **all Filipino nationals** regardless of where the post was made (an OFW in Taiwan was charged for anti-Duterte posts).
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cybercrime_Prevention_Act_of_2012 | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disini_v._Secretary_of_Justice_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### 6.3 The Maria Ressa / Rappler Precedent
***People of the Philippines v. Santos, Ressa and Rappler*** (R-MNL-19-01141-CR), Manila RTC Branch 46, **Judge Rainelda Estacio-Montesa**:
- **Verdict:** June 15, 2020 — **Ressa & Santos convicted of cyberlibel**; Rappler Inc. acquitted (no corporate liability).
- **Sentence:** 6 months to 6 years; **₱400,000** in moral + exemplary damages.
- **Article:** a May 29, 2012 Rappler piece (4 months **before** RA 10175 took effect); DOJ applied a "continuous publication"/"re-publication" theory (a typo correction on Feb. 19, 2014 was treated as republication).
- **Court's reasoning:** Ressa "did not offer a scintilla of proof that they verified the imputations… [Rappler] just simply published them… in reckless disregard." The court drew an **adverse inference** from her refusal to testify (*People v. Resano*).
- **Appeal:** Court of Appeals **upheld** (2022); SC petition pending (UN Special Rapporteur Irene Khan allowed to intervene as *amicus*, Jan. 2024).
- **Reputational impact:** UN, HRW, Amnesty, RSF, EU all condemned; described as **"judicial harassment"** and a **"pattern of intimidation"** against the press.
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People_of_the_Philippines_v._Santos,_Ressa_and_Rappler_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### 6.4 Implications for an AI / KG Platform
1. **Authorship vs. republication:** Mere **re-surfacing or quoting** allegations (especially if unattributed or stripped of "alleged"/context) may be treated as **re-publication** under the Ressa theory. A platform that *generates* summaries characterizing a person could be analogized to an author.
2. **Truth is NOT an absolute defense:** Under RPC Art. 361, truth is a defense **only if shown to have been published with good motives and for a justifiable end**. A platform must therefore preserve **context, attribution, and the "alleged" framing** of unproven impeachment charges.
3. **Fair-report privilege:** RPC Art. 354–355 recognize **privileged communication**, including **fair and true reports of official proceedings** (e.g., a verified impeachment complaint, a Senate trial transcript, an SC decision). The KG platform should **anchor every claim to a primary-source document** and **quote rather than paraphrase** defamatory imputations.
4. **Stricter penalty online:** Cyberlibel (6–12 yrs) > ordinary libel (6 mo–4 yrs). Anything the platform serves via a web/app interface is "via a computer system."
5. **Long prescriptive period:** 12 years for cyberlibel — exposure is durable.
6. **No DMCA-style safe harbor:** PH has no broad platform immunity equivalent to §230; the **E-Commerce Act (RA 8792)** and IA safeguards are narrower.
_Source: synthesis from above + https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cybercrime_Prevention_Act_of_2012_
**Confidence: HIGH** on legal text; **MEDIUM** on safe-harbor characterization (consult PH counsel).

## 7. Data Privacy Implications (RA 10173, Data Privacy Act of 2012)

### 7.1 The Statute & Regulator
- **RA 10173** signed **Aug. 15, 2012**; **Implementing Rules & Regulations (IRR)** signed **Aug. 24, 2016**, effective **Sept. 9, 2016**.
- Influenced by the EU Data Protection Directive and APEC Privacy Framework.
- Regulator: **National Privacy Commission (NPC)**, created under Sec. 7–9, attached to DICT for policy coordination but **independent** in function. Formed **March 7, 2016**.
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Privacy_Commission | https://www.officialgazette.gov.ph/2012/08/15/republic-act-no-10173/_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### 7.2 Key Definitions & Scope Relevant to a KG Platform
- **Personal data** = personal information + sensitive personal information.
- **Sensitive personal information** (Sec. 3[l]) includes: race, age, color, marital status, **SSS/GSIS/TIN numbers**, **health/education/genetic/sexual life**, **government-issued IDs**, licenses, **criminal record**.
- A KG platform ingesting impeachment documents will process **large volumes of sensitive personal data**: SALNs (assets, bank accounts, real property, family members), witness identities, **criminal allegations**, IDs.
_Source: RA 10173 text; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Privacy_Commission_
**Confidence: HIGH**

### 7.3 Lawful Bases for Processing (Sec. 12–13)
The Act provides several bases; the most relevant for an impeachment KG platform:
1. **Consent** (Sec. 12[a]) — impractical at scale for politicians/witnesses.
2. **Contractual necessity** (Sec. 12[b]) — generally inapplicable.
3. **Legal obligation** (Sec. 12[c]) — if processing is required by law.
4. **Vital interests / public interest** (Sec. 12[d]–[e]).
5. **Legitimate interest** (Sec. 13[b], NPC Advisory Opinion 2017-01): the platform may rely on its **legitimate interest** in facilitating transparency/accountability, **provided** the data subject's rights and reasonable expectations are not overridden. This is the **most likely anchor** for a journalism/accountability KG, but it **requires a balancing test** and is **NARROWER than GDPR's legitimate-interest basis** in practice — NPC has been cautious.

**Public-figure / public-office nuance:** Public officers' SALNs and official acts are presumptively **matters of public concern** (Constitution Art. III, Sec. 7 — right to information on matters of public concern). This supports a **public-interest / legitimate-interest** basis for processing their official-act data, but **does NOT automatically extend** to family members' private financial data, witnesses' identities (esp. vulnerable witnesses), or uncorroborated allegations.
_Source: RA 10173 text; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Privacy_Commission | Constitution Art. III Sec. 7_
**Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH** on statute; **MEDIUM** on NPC advisory specifics (consult NPC's current advisory register).

### 7.4 Platform Obligations (Sec. 11, 16–21)
- **Appoint a Data Protection Officer (DPO).**
- **Conduct a Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA)** — mandatory for systems processing sensitive data at scale.
- **Implement reasonable and appropriate organizational, physical, and technical security measures.**
- **Data breach notification** to NPC and affected data subjects within **72 hours** of knowledge (NPC Circular 16-03).
- **Data retention limitation** — keep only as long as necessary for the declared purpose.
- **Data subject rights:** be informed, object, access, rectify, erase/block, data portability, damages.

### 7.5 Cross-Border Transfers (Sec. 21)
- Disclosure to **foreign recipients** requires: data subject consent, **or** the recipient is in a jurisdiction with adequate protection, **or** the processing is necessary for a public-interest purpose. **Confidence: HIGH** (statute text)

### 7.6 NPC Enforcement Posture & Relevant Advisory Opinions
- NPC has actively enforced against data breaches (e.g., **Comeleak** — the 2016 COMELEC data breach investigation).
- NPC has issued **advisory guidelines on:**
  - **Biometrics** (NPC Advisory 2017-01),
  - **Employee monitoring**,
  - **Political campaigning & election-related personal data processing** (highly relevant to a platform covering politicians),
  - **Scraping / automated collection** of personal data — NPC aligns with EU EDPB guidance discouraging scraping of personal data without lawful basis.
- A KG platform that **scrapes** news sites, social media, or government portals to build profiles of politicians/witnesses should expect NPC scrutiny.
_Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Privacy_Commission | NPC website privacy.gov.ph_
**Confidence: MEDIUM** on specific advisory numbering (verify against NPC's current register).

### 7.7 Specific Risk Vectors for an Impeachment KG Platform
| Risk Vector | DPA Concern | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Ingesting SALNs (assets, family, bank accounts) | Sensitive personal info (financial) | Restrict to the official's own data; minimize family data; rely on public-interest basis; honor Ombudsman access rules. |
| Naming witnesses / whistleblowers | Sensitive + risk of harm | Pseudonymize or redact non-public witnesses; apply DPA's "vital interest" exception narrowly. |
| Recording criminal allegations as structured facts | May constitute "processing of criminal record"-type sensitive data | Label all allegation fields as **unproven**; link to the charging document; avoid generating derivative assertions. |
| Republishing defamatory content (libel overlap, §6) | DPA + libel risk compound | Quote, attribute, anchor to primary source, preserve "alleged" framing. |
| Profiling politicians (scoring, scoring risk) | Automated decision-making (Sec. 16[h]) | Provide meaningful human review; avoid solely-automated adverse determinations. |
| Cross-border cloud hosting | Sec. 21 transfer rules | Use adequate-jurisdiction hosting or obtain consent; sign DPAs with processors. |
| Retaining impeachment data indefinitely | Retention limitation | Define retention policy tied to the "accountability/transparency" purpose; allow rectification requests. |
